Img: Seven bridges of Königsberg

In the 18th century, the city of Königsberg (now Kaliningrad) had four land areas connected by seven bridges. People wondered: is it possible to walk through the city, crossing each bridge exactly once?

In 1736, mathematician Leonhard Euler approached the problem in a new way. He represented land areas as points (vertices) and bridges as lines (edges). This abstraction led him to an important point: such a path is only possible if there are zero or two points with an odd number of bridges.

But in Königsberg, all four had an odd number, making the riddle impossible to solve. Euler's work led to the foundation for graph theory, a branch of mathematics that explores how things connect, from cities and networks to social relationships.
